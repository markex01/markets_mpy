Key Takeaways: Data Centres

Data centres are shifting from passive consumers → active energy market participants. Energy is becoming the next vector of vertical integration.

From energy consumers to energy nodes
  • Just as Amazon built AWS for itself and later commercialized it, and as Meta and Google vertically integrated into submarine cables, energy is becoming the next vector of integration.
  • Hyperscalers (Google, Microsoft, Amazon) — especially in the US — are starting to build their own generation, adding on-site storage, and in some cases becoming net sellers to the grid. Europe is moving slower but the direction is the same.
  • The main bottleneck is grid interconnection and time. Three strategic options to overcome it:
    • Pre-secure grid capacity: developers who have done so hold a massive competitive advantage.
    • Load modularity (flexible interconnection): shifting non-critical loads to off-peak periods. A 3–5% reduction at peak (5–9 PM) can unlock significantly more grid access — one US study showed 650 additional connections in the same Ashburn location. Still largely unquantified in Europe.
    • Self-powered microgrids (dispatchable gas + solar/wind + BESS, behind-the-meter) are gaining most traction. The data centre becomes an energy node — a clear opportunity for integrated developers.
  • Fuel cells: Equinix has commissioned 30 installations of 100 MW fuel cells across 20 sites. 20% lower emissions than gas, approaching price competitiveness. Scale is already significant — a market signal worth monitoring.
AI-driven infrastructure shift
  • AI is making traditional in-building UPS systems obsolete. Rising power per rack is pushing data centres toward large external battery systems — a major replacement investment cycle.
  • AI training creates sudden, extreme power swings. The challenge is now power stability, not just supply. Value is migrating from battery cells to advanced power conversion and control systems.
Key Takeaways: BESS Markets

Alpha is no longer in market selection — it is in: grid connection timing · optimizer quality · revenue stack depth. Whoever secures grid connection early and builds a superior revenue stack wins.

Revenue model transition
  • Ancillary revenues compressing across markets. UK has already crashed — the rest of Europe follows with a 1–3 year lag. Arbitrage is taking over as core revenue driver; tolling agreements emerging as financing bridge.
  • Projects underwritten on frequency response assumptions are mispricing risk.
Duration as saturation indicator
  • Duration mix is the clearest market saturation proxy. 1–2h assets = ancillary-driven. Shift toward 4h+ signals arbitrage maturity.
Private hedging products filling the gap
  • Private floors, collars, and merchant revenue guarantees — from capital allocators, not governments — growing rapidly. A genuine gap filled by a small number of sophisticated infrastructure funds. Tracking who offers them and at what cost is strategically relevant.
Co-location with solar PV is not a silver bullet
  • Execution risk is systematically underestimated. Revenue attribution between solar PPAs and BESS dispatch is complex.
Supply chain shift: China repositioning & European manufacturing
  • China tightening battery export policy — higher prices, stricter requirements, fewer fiscal incentives — narrows the cost advantage of Chinese suppliers.
  • Opens space for European manufacturers, especially in BtM and data centre segments where buyers prioritize supply security over lowest capex.
  • BaaS models gaining traction: European financiers funding Chinese equipment deployed locally. Worth monitoring — open questions on performance guarantees and incentive alignment.
